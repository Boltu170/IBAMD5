The Price Mechanism - How Free Markets Are Supposed to Work

  • Last week we studied perfectly competitive markets
  • Price competition is the main way firms compete
  • Three key conditions needed for this to work:

Condition 1: Prices and quantities can change freely

Condition 2: Firms can freely enter or leave markets

‚Ä¢ Amazon HAS market power - it can set prices and push out competitors

In a perfectly competitive market, NO firm has market power. In reality, big firms have enormous market power!

üá¶üáπ The Austrian Theory - Hayek's Big Idea

Friedrich von Hayek (1945) - A Famous Economist's Key Argument

Main idea: You do NOT need to know everything for markets to work.

Prices act like signals that tell you what to do - even without knowing WHY!

Imagine You Run an Electronics Repair Shop...

What happens: A tin factory closes down somewhere in the world

Do you know this? No! You are just a small repair shop owner

What happens next:

1. Tin supply goes down ‚Üí first buyers offer higher prices to get tin

2. Tin price rises ‚Üí soldering material becomes more expensive

3. Your soldering materials become more expensive

4. You notice prices went up and adjust your business

You never needed to know about the factory closing! The price change told you everything you needed to know.

‚Ä¢ Factory costs ¬£100 million to build (fixed cost)

üè≠ Why Big Firms Win - Part 1

Advantage 1: Economies of Scale - Three Sources

a) Fixed Costs

A big expense you pay ONCE, then spread across millions of products

Example: A factory costs ¬£100m. Make 1 million products = ¬£100 cost per product. Make 10 million = ¬£10 per product!

b) Learning by Doing

The more you produce, the more you learn to do it cheaper and faster

Workers get better, processes improve, waste is reduced

c) Division of Labour

With more workers, each person can specialise in ONE task and become expert at it

Result: Faster, cheaper, better production

üöó Henry Ford's Assembly Line - 1913

Henry Ford introduced the assembly line to make the Model T car:

‚Ä¢ Each worker did ONE simple job repeatedly ‚Üí better division of labour

‚Ä¢ More cars produced ‚Üí more learning by doing

‚Ä¢ Assembly line was a huge fixed cost spread over millions of cars

Result: Price of Model T dropped by 2/3! Small firms had NO chance to compete!

Imagine making a sandwich in a cafe:

One person does everything: Takes order, gets bread, cuts bread, adds filling, wraps, takes payment = slow!

Division of labour: Person 1 takes orders, Person 2 cuts bread, Person 3 adds fillings, Person 4 wraps, Person 5 takes payment = much faster!

Each person becomes expert at their ONE task. Big firms can divide labour much more finely than small firms can!

üìà How Markets Become Oligopolies

The Process of Oligopolisation - Step by Step

Step 1: Market starts with many small firms all competing

Step 2: Some firms do better (luck, skill, better ideas) and grow bigger

Step 3: Bigger firms become MORE competitive (economies of scale etc.)

Step 4: They grow even MORE ‚Üí become even more competitive ‚Üí grow even MORE

Step 5: Eventually only a FEW giant firms dominate. Small firms either:

‚Ä¢ Get outcompeted and close down

‚Ä¢ Get bought out (taken over) by big firms

‚Ä¢ Survive in small niches big firms don't care about

üåç The Reality of Modern Markets

The Assumptions of Perfect Competition are BROKEN in Modern Markets

‚ùå Assumption 1 BROKEN: Free price adjustment

Large firms STRONGLY resist price changes. They avoid price competition and control demand as much as possible.

‚ùå Assumption 2 BROKEN: Free entry and exit

Enormous barriers to entry prevent new firms. Suppliers and buyers are often locked in. Consumers are influenced by heavy advertising.

‚ùå Assumption 3 BROKEN: Small firms with no market power

Dominant firms have ENORMOUS market power - over prices, suppliers, customers, and even governments!

üìö John Kenneth Galbraith - "The New Industrial State"

Galbraith was a famous American economist who argued:

‚Ä¢ Modern economies should NOT be called market systems

‚Ä¢ They should be called planning systems

‚Ä¢ Large corporations plan so much of the economy that the "free market" is largely a myth

‚Ä¢ The price mechanism still works to SOME degree

‚Ä¢ But corporate planning is more important than the price mechanism in modern economies

Yes - but less than textbooks suggest!

The price mechanism still exists and still works:

‚Ä¢ Price changes still send signals to market actors

‚Ä¢ Some markets are still fairly competitive (like local services)

‚Ä¢ Price competition happens in some areas

BUT overall, corporate planning by large firms is MORE important in determining what gets produced, at what price, and in what quantities.

Real economies are a mix of markets and planning - dominated by the planning of large corporations!

SMEs compete on price, quality, location, time, and differentiation. Even small firms have SOME market power.

3. Large Firms Beat Small Firms (Small vs Big)

Economies of scale, R&D, branding, risk management, supplier power, political power. Large firms overwhelmingly more competitive.

4. Oligopolies Form and Stay Stable

Markets become dominated by a few giant firms. Barriers to entry keep new firms out. Structure is very stable once formed.

5. Oligopolistic Competition (Big vs Big)

Big firms AVOID price wars. Compete through innovation, new markets, branding. Need stability for long-term planning. Control supply and demand.

6. Reality of Modern Markets (Galbraith)

All three assumptions of perfect competition are broken. Modern economies are corporate PLANNING systems, not free markets.

üéØ The Big Takeaway

The economy you learned about in basic theory (many small firms, free prices, free entry) does NOT describe the real world.

The real world is dominated by a handful of giant corporations that plan carefully, avoid price competition, control their suppliers and customers, and shape the rules through political power.
